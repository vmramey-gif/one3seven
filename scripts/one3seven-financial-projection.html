Why workers are free

Worker-side is free to drive supply and keep intake volume high. The firm pays for access to pre-analyzed, document-backed intakes. This is identical to Clio's model (firms pay, clients are free) and LegalMatch's lead model. Free worker side removes friction, scales the pipeline.

Future revenue layers

Year 3+: Per-intake success fee on accepted cases (1–2% of settlement referral). Year 5+: HR compliance intelligence layer for enterprise employers. Year 7+: White-label licensing to legal aid organizations and bar associations.

Why legal SaaS retains well

Once an attorney firm integrates an intake workflow into their practice, switching cost is high — client data, intake history, and established process live on the platform. Clio's NPS is consistently 70+. MyCase was acquired for $193M on the back of sticky firm relationships. Legal software churn is structurally lower than general B2B SaaS because the workflow is mission-critical and daily.

Comparable exit data

MyCase acquired for ~$193M — Year 10 legal SaaS firm. Filevine raised $108M at implied valuation of $500M+. Clio valued at $1.6B with $150M+ ARR. The legal SaaS exit environment is proven and active.

The disciplined path

Reach break-even at Year 4 without external funding. Use Years 1–3 to build the network effect that makes one3Seven defensible. Raise only when growth capital can compound returns — not to cover operating losses indefinitely.

The one number that matters most

At 5,400 firms paying an average of $607/month, one3Seven generates $39M in ARR — from a market of 15,000–20,000 plaintiff-side employment firms, representing 27–36% penetration. The unit economics, retention benchmarks, and comparable exit data all point to the same conclusion: this is a category worth building, and the window to own it is now.
