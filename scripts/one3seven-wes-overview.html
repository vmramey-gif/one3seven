The Problem & The Fix · 02
The Problem
Cases die before they start.
Every day, workers with legitimate employment claims — wrongful termination, wage theft, retaliation — arrive at attorney offices with documents scattered across phones, emails, and folders. Attorneys spend 4–6 hours per intake just getting organized before they can even evaluate the case. When intake fails, the worker loses their shot at justice. The attorney loses a case they never got to see.
⏱
$1,400–$2,100 wasted per intake
4–6 hours of attorney or paralegal time at $350/hr — just getting documents organized before the case evaluation even begins.
⏳
180-day EEOC window
Workers have 180 days from termination to file with the EEOC. Without organized intake, this deadline is frequently missed — the case disappears before anyone fights it.
📁
Critical documents go missing
Pay stubs, HR complaints, text messages — the evidence that wins cases never makes it to counsel in an organized form. 40% of cases are lost before the first consult.
🔍
No dominant AI solution exists
Legal AI is racing into contract review, research, and drafting. Workers' rights intake — the front door to employment law — has no organized AI player. The lane is open.
The Solution
one3Seven organizes the intake.
The attorney evaluates the case.
Workers upload their documents. AI extracts a timestamped timeline, identifies retaliation patterns, calculates EEOC deadlines, surfaces key quotes, and flags missing documents. The attorney receives a structured, attorney-ready intake card — before the consultation begins. No legal conclusions. No case scoring. Just the work that used to cost 4–6 hours, done in minutes.

How It Works
Three steps. Both sides served.
1
Worker uploads
Pay stubs, emails, HR complaints, texts, screenshots — anything. Guided intake flow, no legal knowledge needed. Mobile-first.

Timeline extracted. Dates confirmed. Retaliation patterns flagged. EEOC deadline calculated. Key quotes surfaced. Documents categorized.

Structured intake card arrives in firm dashboard. Attorney evaluates the case — not the documents. Worker controls when to share.
